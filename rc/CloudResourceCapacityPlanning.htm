Exercise 1 - Cloud Capacity Planning Factors

It is important to consider that there is always a cost associated with your resources, whether working on-premises or in public cloud infrastructure.

To measure the potential costs of cloud infrastructure, it is key for organizations to plan what capacity they will require before building resources to ensure that the costs are the most reasonable for the business and fall into agreed Return on Investment (RoI) budgets.

Task 1 - Investigate Microsoft Azure Pricing Calculator

Creating resources using a cloud provider always involves a cost. Companies need to assess the costs involved when planning to migrate resources to a CSP.

Upon creation of a resource, the subscription will be charged according to the consumption on a pay-as-you-go billing methodology.

In this task, the Azure pricing calculator will be used to investigate the costing involved in the hosting of resources on the Azure platform.

Step 1

Connect to PLABWIN10.

Step 2

From the Taskbar, click on the Microsoft Edge icon.

Step 3

Click into the web address bar and type:

azure pricing calculator

Press Enter.

Step 4

Click on the Pricing Calculator | Azure web link, which is typically found as the first search result.

Step 5

The Pricing Calculator page is displayed.

Click Accept on the cookies message.

Note: If an online survey pop-up should appear, click No.

Step 6

Scroll down the Azure | Pricing calculator webpage and select Virtual Machines.

Step 7

When the Virtual Machine added pop-up window appears, click View. Alternatively, scroll to the bottom of the webpage to get to the Virtual Machines section.

Note: The prices shown on this website may differ from the screenshots.

Step 8

From the Virtual Machines section of the webpage, click on the Region dropdown and change the selection to UK South.

Note: The different regions in which resources can be created in Azure is displayed. The region can be changed according to your location. For the purpose of the exercise, the UK South region will be used.

Step 9

Change the Instance dropdown to D8d v4: 8vCPU(s), 32 GB RAM, 300 GB temporary storage.

Note: Different Virtual machine sizes have different costs. A company can use the pricing calculator to estimate the amount a specific virtual machine will cost per month, depending on the resource utilization.

Step 10

Scroll to the bottom of the webpage to the Estimated upfront cost section.

Click the CURRENCY dropdown and change the selection to United Kingdom - Pound (£) GBP.

Note: The Azure pricing calculator's currency can be changed to better understand the cost involved when creating resources on the Azure platform. For this task, it will be changed to British pounds.

Step 11

Notice the VM upfront and monthly costs have been calculated and shown in British pounds.

Keep PLABWIN10 in its current state and move to the next task.

Exercise 2 - Cloud Density, Load, Trends and Performance Planning

Alongside Capacity Planning, it is also imperative to consider what performance is expected from a resource or infrastructure being developed on the cloud. It is easy in business environments to focus so much on Capacity planning that Resource planning takes a hit or vice-versa, but it is important to find a balance between the two.

An infrastructure you have been instructed to build needs to have the resources available to meet the needs and objectives of the organization while at the same time staying within agreed baselines so as not to exceed budget and build resources that will be underutilized.

Task 1 - Investigate Resource Planning in Microsoft Azure

After using different tools to investigate budgeting requirements for both AWS and Microsoft Azure, there are also considerations to take into account when building resources in a public cloud.

In this task, we will investigate which specifications can impact resource planning when creating a Virtual Machine (VM) in Microsoft Azure.

Step 1

Connect to PLABWIN10.

Step 2

Click on the Microsoft Edge icon pinned to the Taskbar.

Note: When opening the Microsoft Edge browser, it will automatically redirect to the Azure Portal and sign in. An Azure subscription will be provided for all lab activities.

Step 3

The Azure portal page will appear. Click the Create a resource button.

Step 4

The Create a resource page will appear.

From the Categories menu on the left-hand side of the page, select Compute.

Step 5

On the Compute section, scroll down the list to Virtual machine and click Create.

Step 6

This will open the Create a virtual machine portal.

Click the Resource Group dropdown menu and select the pre-populated resource group.

Note: When creating resources in a real-world environment, you may have to create a new RG to “bundle” all extra resources as part of your infrastructure into one place. For example, when creating a Virtual Machine, you will also create a Virtual Network, Virtual Network Adaptor (or VNIC) and a Subnet which all have their associated costs, and with multiple RGs, you can analyze costs based on each specific resource. Note that inside the lab environment, you will be unable to create a new RG.

Step 7

Scroll down the page and click on the Image drop-down menu.

Ensure that Ubuntu Server 20.04 LTS - Gen 2 is selected.

Note: This is also an important factor of what will ultimately affect the cost of the infrastructure, as some operating systems can be more cost-effective than others, but the OS you choose should ultimately depend on the infrastructure you are looking to create.
You should consider what role a device should have when choosing an operating system. For example, for a low-utilization device, an older version of Microsoft Server or a gen 1 image may be sufficient, provided it meets security requirements, or the most recent version may also be required if the opposite is true.

Step 8

Again, under the Image section, click the Configure VM Generation weblink.

Note: Immediately when selecting a Resource Group, a policy will apply to state that the currently selected size is not compatible. That is because Ubuntu Server 20.04 LTS - Gen2 does not support the A1_v2 SKU Size and has been prohibited. In a real-world scenario, which policies are applied, and which OSs are supported will differ.

Step 9

This will open the Configure VM generation pane. In this pane, select Generation 1.

Click Apply.

Note: In Azure, Gen 1 images support most operating systems, and Gen 2 images support most 64-bit versions of Windows, Linux and FreeBDS operating systems. Now that you have selected Gen 1, the error message that appears under the Size section will vanish.

Step 10

Scroll down to the Size setting and click the See all sizes weblink.

Step 11

This will bring you to the Select a VM size portal.

Expand the A-Series v2 dropdown.

Note: When considering performance planning for resources in a public infrastructure, it is imperative that the correct sizes are used when creating new resources. Typically, in a real-world scenario, you will have specific requirements for what is needed from each resource, and you must ensure that this resource can meet these requirements. Some public cloud resources allow you to scale these resources as required. SKU sizes have been limited to Standard_A1_v2 for Generation 1 images in the lab environment.

Step 12

Click on the first entry on the list, A1_V2.

Note: As you can see in the list, an A1_V2 SKU for Ubuntu Server 20.04 includes 1 vCPU (or Virtual CPU, meaning that these are billed according to the number of cores used or Core-based billing).
Prices of VMs also increase with the amount of RAM, storage disks and other metrics that determine the raw power of the VM, so in a real-world environment, it is key to only use resources that are required for your infrastructure, or you could risk going over budget.

Step 13

At the top of the page, click on the Create a virtual machine breadcrumb web link to be taken back to the Create a virtual machine portal.

Step 14

Scroll back up to the top of the page and click the Networking tab.

Step 15

This will bring you to the Networking page.

Note: As discussed above, you can see that the virtual machine portal will also create a relevant subnet and virtual network, both of which have their associated costs. Although comparatively a small cost, these should be considered when creating resources.

Step 16

At the top of the page, click the Management tab.

Step 17

From the Management page, you will see options of which services have been enabled on the VM before it is created.

Note: Here, you could enable or disable a managed storage account or allocate a login to this device, linking it up with Azure Active Directory (AD), again, which has its own costs.

At the top of the page, click the Microsoft Azure button to be brought back to the Azure Dashboard.

Click OK.

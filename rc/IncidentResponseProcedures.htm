Exercise 1 - Incident Response Planning

As organizations grow, it is important for IT staff to plan ahead and be strategic for cases where incidents may happen that threaten infrastructure and a business running smoothly. When planning for these incidents, it is imperative to consider anything that may put an infrastructure at risk of failure and have an appropriate response to remediate the issue rather than working reactionarily, which typically leads to more downtime.

This becomes more important and equally difficult to gauge as infrastructures become larger and more intricate. For example, an infrastructure engineer may push an update through to a live environment (that safely passes through dev and QA environments), only to find that it causes an effect on a legacy resource. In the worst cases, where that resource was a Single Point of Failure (SPOF), this can bring down the entire infrastructure.

Task 1 - Review a Template Incident Response Documentation

Various documents comprise incident response planning, and the exact documentation will vary from business to business.

In this task, we will review some example documents that can be used in incident response planning.

Step 1

Connect to PLABWIN10.

Step 2

From the Taskbar, click File Explorer.

Step 3

In the File Explorer window, under Quick access, click Documents.

Double-click the Incident Response Planning Template Excel file.

Step 4

Close the Microsoft Office Professional Plus 2016 Wizard.

The Microsoft Excel file will open on the Threat Categorization Matrix tab.

Here, you can see an example of different threat classifications based on the probability of the issue occurring vs. the impact it would have on an infrastructure.

The exact specifications here will change from business to business, although there are four categories here, for example: Low, Medium, High, and Priority 1 High.

Step 5

Click on the Monitoring Plan tab.

Step 6

This tab displays a simple monitoring sheet that outlines different resources that comprise a wider infrastructure, including the resource description, a judgment of risk, backup details, and if it is part of a wider infrastructure or a stand-alone resource.

Step 7

Scroll to the right of the page to view the remaining data.

You can see multiple potential concerns.

For example, PLABKV01, an Azure Key Vault, does not have any infrastructure relying on it directly (as noted in column G) and therefore has been declared as not a risk, although it has not been backed up for a year and a half before the other resources.

This could indicate a SPOF, as the Key Vault could store business-critical data.

Step 8

Finally, click the Call Tree & Decision Plan tab.

Step 9

This tab outlines a blank call tree, in which different hierarchal levels are shown with fields for contact details.

It outlines a plan of whom to communicate with in case of an incident and could also potentially double up as an outline of roles and responsibilities.

In the given example, the Director may be the person who can authorize decisions.

Step 10

Close the Microsoft Excel window by clicking the Close button.
